"I've finished," Aggie said. "And there's something I want to show you. Something that doesn't quite sit right."

Jack looked up.

"Albert and Dorothy Abbott. Indentured to Alpha-7, transported there from a QuantumGrid facility. You knew the first part; I found the rest in the transport records." A pause. "But there's a third piece. When GigaSource issued the loan that ruined them, they ran an identity check first. CyberNexus performed it. The check passed – but the system flagged it. Minimal verification depth. Identities confirmed through the SnapMesh registry and nothing else. No secondary sources. No employment history before their QuantumGrid placement. Fourteen months of records and not a day more."

"What does that mean?"

"On its own? Perhaps nothing. People with thin records exist – the poor, the transient, those who've moved often." Aggie paused, and there was something measured in it. "But it's unusual, Jack. Most lives leave a longer trail than fourteen months. CyberNexus noticed it too – flagged the file and disclaimed all responsibility. GigaSource approved the loan anyway, which is the only part that doesn't surprise me."

Jack almost smiled. Almost.

"So what are you saying?"

"I'm saying the records of your parents are unusually shallow, and a corporate system noticed it before I did. That's all I can say with certainty – I won't speculate beyond the data." A pause. "This is the complete picture as the records hold it. Three threads, and they all stop where they stop." Another pause. "I'll keep looking. I don't expect more – I've followed every line, and they end. But the shallowness troubles me, and I'll keep watching. If anything more exists, I'll find it eventually."
