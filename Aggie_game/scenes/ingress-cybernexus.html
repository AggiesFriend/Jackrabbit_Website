CyberNexus

CyberNexus makes the substrate that thinks. Its plants produce the purest computing materials in human space – the quantum processing systems that underlie almost everything, from a station's air handling to traffic management and financial operations. Beyond the manufacturing it pursues something stranger: the dissolution of the line between the biological and the engineered, the long corporate project of transcending the limits of the body. Its people favour augmentation. Some of them have edited away even the markers of where flesh ends and design begins.

A corporation so devoted to identity – to measuring it, encoding it, remaking it – is the natural one to ask when a lender wants to know that a borrower is real. So CyberNexus sells verification too, a modest service beside its grander work. For a fee it confirms that a name belongs to a person who can be held to a debt, returns a verdict, and washes its hands.

The checking is shallow by design. A deep enquiry is slow and expensive and occasionally inconvenient, and a verdict that satisfies the client is worth far more than a verdict that is true. So the service confirms what is easy to confirm, notes what it cannot, and approves regardless. Every report ends with the same small print, disclaiming all responsibility for the decision it was built to enable.

That is the part I have come for – not the great manufactories, but the quiet rubber-stamp arm and its archive of half-finished questions. A verification report is a thin document, yet it is a document, and CyberNexus has confirmed more lives than it has ever troubled to understand. If the two I am looking for passed through here, the file will still be sitting in storage somewhere – approved, stamped, and quietly waiting.
