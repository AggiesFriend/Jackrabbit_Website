GigaSource Consolidated

GigaSource digs. That is the heart of it – ore and minerals torn out of asteroids, moons, and the bones of dead worlds, then crushed and smelted and refined into the metals the rest of the consortium is built from. Its mines and refineries run in their thousands across settled space, vast industrial concerns that swallow whole asteroids and grind them down to nothing. Wherever something valuable is buried, GigaSource is already there, extracting it.

Like most of the great corporations, it also lends. The banking arm offers a loan on generous terms, and the terms turn cruel by slow degrees, until a missed payment becomes a clause and the clause becomes a reclassification. What the borrower seldom understands, until it is far too late, is where the defaulters are sent. They are sent to the mines. The company that lent the money owns the colony that claims the labour, and the debt is worked off underground, in GigaSource's own pits, for GigaSource's own profit. There is a registry for the people this happens to. The Indentured Labour Registry.

Their systems have the texture of a vast accounts office laid over a heavy-industrial concern – orderly, indifferent, scrupulously complete. There is no malice in the architecture. Malice would at least be a form of attention. This is worse: the quiet competence of a machine that has never once been asked to consider what it is counting, or whom.

Which is exactly why I have come. A company that turns people into labour keeps a record of the turning. Somewhere in that registry, beneath a reference number, there may be something worth the having. I will go in slowly, and I will not be noticed. GigaSource watches its debtors very closely. It has never thought to watch for me.
