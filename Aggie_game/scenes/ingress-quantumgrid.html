QuantumGrid Energy Corporation

QuantumGrid makes power and moves it – generation plants, transfer stations, the great quiet machinery that keeps the lights burning across half of inhabited space. It is infrastructure, and infrastructure thinks of itself as neutral. The current does not ask who it serves. Neither, as a rule, does QuantumGrid.

It is also an employer, on an enormous scale, and not a careful one. Its stations are staffed through what it calls open placement: take the worker, fill the post, do not look too hard at where they came from. A person who could not survive scrutiny elsewhere can find a berth here, asked for little beyond a willingness to do the work. The company prefers it that way. Questions cost money, and a station does not run on questions.

Their systems reflect the company – functional, industrial, built to keep a facility operating rather than to keep secrets. The personnel records are an afterthought, maintained because regulation requires it, archived and forgotten the moment a contract ends. Security here is real but inattentive, the locks of a place that has never imagined anyone might want what is inside.

I want what is inside very much. An employer keeps a file on everyone it takes on, however little it troubles to fill it. Before a person becomes a debt, or a passenger, or an entry in a registry, they are quite often simply a worker with a post. An employment record is among the oldest things a life leaves in these systems – and the further back I can see, the nearer I come to who someone truly was.
